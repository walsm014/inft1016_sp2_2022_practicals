Introduction to the iPhone

The iPhone was first announced by Steve Jobs at 2007’s Macworld conference, and would go on to become one of the most influential pieces of technology in history. The product featured a touch-activated 3.5-inch screen, similar to that of their previous iPod releases, and users would be able to make calls, play games and type messages with the tap of a finger. The iPhones that followed (iPhone 3G onwards) would have 3G, 4G and 5G wireless Internet capabilities over time, meaning users could browse online as much as they wanted, wherever they were (Mickalowski et al., 2008). This was a major development in mobile phone history.

What made the iPhone Stand Out?/iOS - iPhone’s Specialized Operating System

Users loved that the iPhone served as a camera, gaming device, GPS, alarm clock, Internet browser and mobile phone all in one. The different possibilities the device offered provided instant gratification to users as they had access to entertainment and social interactions on demand. The device became so popular, so quickly, that even teachers and students in medical practice were using them to enhance their learning on patient care (Wallace, Clark & White, 2012). With the iPhone (along with other touch- screen Apple products such as the iPad and iPod) came iOS, Apple’s touch-screen device operating system. Whilst the first version of iOS served as a basic support for the iPhone, the second version was an extreme breakthrough for smart technology as a whole, showing off an Apple-specific App Store with over 500 apps and a Maps app with walking directions to different locations. As the version history increased, the iPhone’s app displays changed, voice recognition called Siri along with new apps were released, storage solutions such as iCloud emerged, and card-based payment options such as Apple Pay were released (Costello, 2022).

How has the iPhone solved real-world problems/How does it help people?

There is a plethora of real-world problems that the iPhone solved, and continue to solve to this day with newer, updated releases. Some researchers argue that smartphones cause humans to be ‘cognitive misers’, seeking out quick solutions by making the smartphone do their thinking for them (Barr et al., 2015), however, smartphones are a fantastic way to work smarter and not harder when wanting to find out information quickly. Concepts such as voice-recognition and tap-buttons to type are also an accessible alternative for people with disabilities, who may struggle with physical writing or typing on a computer. For regular users of the device, smartphones really are an extension of oneself and a part of one’s identity (Park & Kaye, 2018).
